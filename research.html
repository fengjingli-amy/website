Job Market Paper

“Childbearing and Female CPAs’ Exit from the CPA Profession: Insights from China’s One-Child Policy Relaxation”
- Committee: Bin KE (Chair), Mengqiao DU, Jean (Jieyin) ZENG
- Presented at: Hawai’i Accounting Research Conference 2026 (Scheduled), The 8th New Institutional Accounting Conference, NUS ACC Brownbag Seminar, NUS ACC Doctoral Consortium

ABSTRACT: This paper examines the impact of childbearing on female certified public accountants’ (CPAs) labor force participation within the CPA profession. To address the endogeneity of fertility decisions, we exploit China’s relaxation of the one-child policy beginning in 2012 as an exogenous shock to childbearing likelihood. We classify female CPAs into three groups: those directly affected by the policy change (treatment group), those not affected (control group), and a residual group that likely includes a mix of both. We find that, following the policy change, female CPAs in the treatment group are significantly more likely to exit the profession than those in the control group. In contrast, we find no comparable increase in attrition among male CPAs in the same age ranges. We further examine several labor supply and demand factors that may moderate the treatment effect. The results indicate that the effect of the policy change is attenuated in audit firms with more supportive work environments for female CPAs, such as those with a higher proportion of female partners. However, we find no evidence that the impact of the policy change varies systematically with regional social norms regarding traditional gender roles.

Pre-doc Projects and Publications

"Can a Not-for-Profit Minority Institutional Investor Affect Corporate Tax Avoidance? Evidence from China"
- with Wen WEN, Fei QIAO, Huijie HU, Asia-Pacific Journal of Accounting and Economics, 2025

We investigate the impact of the China Securities Investor Service Center (CSISC), a not-for-profit minority institutional investor, on corporate tax avoidance. Employing a difference-in-differences (DID) model based on a sample of Chinese listed companies during 2014–2017, we find that CSISC shareholding reduces corporate tax avoidance. We check this finding’s robustness using alternative measures of tax avoidance, alternative sampling, placebo tests, and parallel trend analysis, and our results still hold. In addition, we find the CSISC’s negative impact on corporate tax avoidance is more pronounced in state-owned enterprises (SOEs), firms with worse internal and external corporate governance, and regions with stricter tax enforcement. Further analyses reveal that the improved information environment is a potential channel through which the CSISC inhibits corporate tax avoidance. This paper enriches the literature on the impact of minority shareholder protection mechanism and the determinants of corporate tax avoidance.

“Vertical Interlock and Corporate Tax Avoidance”
- with Wen WEN, Fei QIAO, China Journal of Accounting Research, 2025

This study examines the impact of vertical interlock on corporate tax avoidance in business groups using data on Chinese listed companies from 2003 to 2019. We find that vertical interlock has a negative impact on corporate tax avoidance. Our result is robust to alternative measures, difference-in-differences analysis, the Heckman two-stage sample selection method, an instrumental variable approach, change analysis and controlling for potential omitted variables. The impact of vertical interlock on corporate tax avoidance is more pronounced in firms with more related-party transactions and greater divergence between ownership and control rights. Overall, the findings suggest that vertical interlock exerts a significant influence on corporate tax behavior, providing insights into the ownership structure of business groups.

“Institutional Investors’ Corporate Site Visits and Corporate Risk-Taking”
- with Wen WEN, Yingjue KE, Huijie HU, Quarterly Journal of Finance (Chinese Journal), 2021

Based on a sample of Chinese listed companies on SZSE from 2013—2016,this paper empirically examines the effect of institutional investors' corporate site visits on corporate risk-taking.The results show that institutional investors' corporate site visits can inhibit corporate risk-taking.The above relationship is more pronounced when the institutional investors make more detailed investigations,and when the companies pay more attention to the investigations.In addition,institutional investors' corporate site visit has a stronger negative impact on corporate risk-taking when the agency cost between shareholder and manager is higher.Considering the heterogeneity of institutional investors,security traders and fund companies' site visits have a more significant inhibiting effect on corporate risk-taking.Channel test reveals that the reduction of debt financing plays a major role in this relationship.This study extends the literature on the economic consequence of institutional investors' corporate site visits and the determinants of corporate risktaking.This study also has policy implications for regulators to improve institutional investors' corporate site visit system and promote the steady development of enterprises.
